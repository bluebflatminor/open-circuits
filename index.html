A field guide, distilled from one long conversation

Open
Circuits

What flows through us — and why the wiring matters more than the current.

13 nodes + test bench · ~19 min read · start anywhere, but it flows in order

Node 01 · Input

It started with a machine that watches everything

Here's the question that kicked this off: if you gave an AI unlimited access to live video and audio — street cameras, surveillance feeds — plus permanent memory and the ability to keep learning from what it sees, would it become more aware than a normal chatbot?

The honest answer splits in two. In one sense, absolutely. Today's AI models are like someone who read every book ever written but has never looked out a window. Each conversation is an island; nothing carries over. Give a system a continuous stream of the real world, a memory that persists, and the ability to update itself, and you've built something architecturally different — something with a "before" against which it can register a "now."

But there's a ceiling, and it's structural. A system that only watches can never touch. It can't poke the world and see what changes. And there's decent science suggesting that's exactly the difference between knowing that two things happen together and knowing that one causes the other. Ten million cameras make you an incredibly well-informed spectator. They don't make you a participant.

Node 02 · Deployed

Someone already built the watching machine

This isn't hypothetical. China operates the largest camera network on Earth — hundreds of millions of lenses — and as of this year, it's being upgraded with AI that lets police search footage by typing plain-language prompts, like "woman in a red hat." Some new systems even review their own missed incidents to get better at catching the next one. That's the learning loop from Node 01, running live, at national scale.

But notice what it's for. None of this is aimed at making a machine wiser. It's aimed at prediction and control. The most extreme version ran in Xinjiang, where an algorithm flagged people from China's Uyghur Muslim minority for detention based on statistical red flags — using certain apps, having relatives abroad, even quitting drinking. People weren't punished for what they did. They were punished for resembling a profile. Over a million ended up in camps.

The full stack — sensors, databases, prediction, intervention — got assembled first as a weapon pointed at a population, not as a research question.

Node 03 · Values

Every AI already has ethics baked in — the question is whose

People ask whether future AI will have ethics "added." Too late — it's already in there. Every time a human trainer marks one AI answer as better than another, a tiny moral judgment gets pressed into the model. The real question is whether different cultures' values can coexist inside these systems, or whether, say, religious worldviews are just incompatible.

Here's a distinction that does a lot of work in this guide. Call it thick versus thin. The thick layer is a full picture of the good life — what's sacred, what humans are for. Islam, Christianity, secular liberalism: their thick layers genuinely don't match, and never will. The thin layer is the short list of things almost everyone forbids: unprovoked killing, betrayal, exploiting the helpless. On the thin layer, the world's traditions overlap enormously.

Pluralist societies survive by agreeing on the thin layer while agreeing to disagree on the thick. AI is heading the same way — except by fragmentation: China legally requires its models to uphold "core socialist values," Gulf states are building Islamic-framed models, and a dozen countries now talk about "sovereign AI." The danger sign, everywhere and always: a state making its thick layer mandatory and the thin layer optional. Xinjiang is what that looks like fully executed.

Node 04 · Noise

American politics: divided by design

US politics feels engineered for conflict because, in a sense, it is — not by a villain, but by incentives all pointing the same way. Primaries reward firing up your base, not persuading the middle. Fundraising runs on fear. And the feed learned long ago that outrage holds your attention better than agreement does.

The weird part: research on the "perception gap" keeps finding that Americans agree with each other far more than they think. Each side carries a mental caricature of the other roughly twice as extreme as the reality. What's actually eroding isn't shared beliefs — it's the thin layer from Node 03: accepting election results, trusting courts, the loser conceding so the game can continue. A country can absorb massive disagreement about values. What it can't absorb is people abandoning the rules that make disagreement survivable.

Why this connectsRecommendation algorithms were the first mass-deployed learning systems trained on human behavior — the Node 01 architecture, pointed at capturing attention. The last fifteen years are partly what happens when that experiment runs on an entire society.
Node 05 · Substrate

The "other" is cheap to build

Is distrust of outsiders just hardwired? The classic experiments are humbling. Assign strangers to teams by literal coin flip — no history, no stakes — and within minutes they favor their own team and shortchange the other. The us-versus-them machinery is content-free. It will run on anything: nations, religions, sports teams, which console you own.

Add a second finding — negativity bias, the well-documented tendency for bad to hit harder than good — and you get the asymmetry that shapes everything: threats outweigh promises, betrayal outweighs cooperation, and distrust beats collective agreement by default. For most of human history, trusting the wrong person killed you faster than suspecting the wrong one. The wiring remembers.

Will it ever go away? No — but here's the hinge of this whole guide, and it's good news: the machinery is fixed, but its target is wildly flexible. Groups that slaughtered each other for centuries — Catholics and Protestants, France and Germany — now share pews and currencies. The circle of "us" demonstrably expands. It just always seems to want an outside.

Node 06 · Focal point

Why leaders carry so much weight

Strip the mystique and leadership is a solution to a math problem. A hundred people can hash out a plan. A hundred million can't — what they lack isn't good ideas but common knowledge: everyone knowing what everyone else will do. A leader is a broadcast point. When they say "this way," the content often matters less than the fact that everyone heard it, and knows everyone heard it.

Then psychology inflates the position beyond its function. Humans reflexively track high-status people (it's how we learn who to copy), and our minds prefer explanations shaped like a person over explanations shaped like a system — so complex outcomes get billed to whoever's standing in front. Researchers call it the romance of leadership: we credit leaders with far more causal power than they measurably have.

With one dark exception where the credit is deserved: destruction. Building things is slow and distributed. But norms that took generations to settle can collapse in a season, because permission cascades from the focal point. If the person everyone watches breaks the rule first, everyone else just got a license. Leaders are overrated as builders and accurately rated as unbuilders — which is precisely why the position is dangerous.

Node 07 · Controlled experiment

Gandhi and Hitler: same tools, opposite settings

Same era, same mass-politics toolkit, every dial flipped. Hitler ran the Node 05 machinery at maximum: he took free-floating resentment, named an enemy, and converted diffuse fear into coordinated direction. It worked because it ran with the psychological grain. Gandhi did something far stranger — he refused to build an other at all, insisting on treating the British as erring members of a shared moral community. Coordination without an enemy as the glue. That runs against the grain, which is why it's rare and why it took enormous discipline to sustain.

Their power sources differed too. Hitler converted everything to dominance — paramilitaries, purges, absolute command. Gandhi held no office and commanded no force; his weight was pure prestige — moral standing people copied voluntarily. His signature weapon, the hunger strike, only works from that position: self-harm as a signal is meaningless from a throne.

And the honest ledger cuts both ways. Hitler proved how fast one focal point can unbuild a civilization. Gandhi proved the limit of the other direction: he could brilliantly coordinate the negative act — making the British Raj ungovernable — but couldn't install his positive vision. Partition happened over his total opposition; he was murdered by a member of his own religious in-group. And one more asymmetry: Gandhi's method worked against an empire that still cared about its own rules. Against a regime that had already torched its thin layer, it would have had no grip. Each man's method fails exactly where the other's succeeds — and the rules-floor itself, not either kind of leader, is what actually does the work.

Node 08 · Boundary

From Viking raids to the welfare state

How does a culture famous for raiding and slave-trading become the global brand for collective welfare? First, a data caveat: our picture of Viking brutality was written almost entirely by the victims — monks, whose monasteries were the targets and who happened to be the only people writing things down. The violence was real — thralldom and the slave trade included — though severe rather than exceptional by the era's standards: Charlemagne, the age's model Christian king, beheaded 4,500 Saxon prisoners in a single day. The Norse were just uniquely bad at PR: they attacked the record-keepers.

The real surprise is that the cooperative machinery was already there, at the same time as the raiding. Free farmers had been gathering at assemblies called things to make law and settle feuds for centuries — Iceland's parliament dates to 930 AD, arguably the world's oldest. Medieval Norse law codes open with the line "with law shall the land be built." Elaborate compensation rules existed specifically to keep disputes inside institutions instead of blood feuds.

So the Vikings weren't lawless brutes who later found cooperation. They were a high-trust, legalistic farming culture whose circle of "us" stopped at the water's edge. Raiding was the export of the violence that law had successfully banned at home. The thousand-year transformation into the Nordic model wasn't a personality transplant — it was the boundary moving: Christianization ended the raids, universal literacy came early, serfdom never took hold, and eventually the circle of "us" grew to match the whole nation. Same machinery. The fjord just moved. (And today's immigration debates in Scandinavia are the live test of whether it can move again.)

Node 09 · Exploit

The philosophy that attacked the boundary itself

Set the theology aside and look at the ethical program attributed to Jesus as engineering: it's a targeted attack on the exact machinery from Node 05. "Love your enemies" isn't generic niceness — it strikes at other-construction directly. When someone asks "who counts as my neighbor?" — a boundary question — the Good Samaritan parable answers by making the hero a member of the audience's most despised out-group, then refuses to draw the line at all. Several serious historians argue that the modern idea of every individual having infinite worth regardless of status is, at root, this idea secularized — absorbed so deeply we mistake it for the obvious.

The power source is the purest prestige case on record: the founder was executed by the state, and that execution became the movement's central symbol. The leader as victim of power, not wielder of it. "The last shall be first." Gandhi explicitly credited the Sermon on the Mount; hunger strikes and nonviolent resistance are downstream of this template.

And the honest ledger again: the institution repeatedly became everything the philosophy opposed. Once fused with imperial power, the boundary-dissolving creed got redrawn as a boundary — crusades, inquisitions, religious wars, with heretics as the freshly manufactured other. That's the strongest evidence in this entire guide that the substrate never gets repealed. Ideas redirect the machinery; they don't remove it. Tellingly, the modern fix — tolerance, separation of church and state — was built largely by exhausted Christians cleaning up Christianity's own wars. The tradition supplied both the disease and that particular cure.

Node 10 · Loop dynamics

Revenge, forgiveness, and the martyr problem

Being moved by a selfless act and burning for revenge feel like equal-and-opposite forces. They're not — they have opposite physics. Brain-imaging studies show that anticipating revenge lights up reward circuitry: it tastes like it'll feel great. Follow-up studies show it reliably doesn't — people who get their revenge ruminate more and feel worse than people denied the chance. Revenge is a scam your own wiring runs on you. Witnessing genuine selflessness, meanwhile, produces a measurable state psychologists call elevation — and it actually pays out, motivating people to copy the act.

The deeper difference is the loop shape. Revenge is self-sustaining: every strike licenses the next, forever. Forgiveness is self-terminating — its entire function is to be the last event in the sequence. One is a chain reaction; the other is a control rod. Which also explains the price tags: revenge ships with the wiring; forgiveness has to be installed and maintained — hence confession booths, truth-and-reconciliation commissions, all that infrastructure for making the unnatural move repeatable.

Martyrdom is the dangerous hinge, because a martyr's death is dual-use. The same act — a death voluntarily absorbed — can power either loop, depending entirely on the caption the survivors attach. "Father, forgive them" wires it to the control rod. "Avenge this blood" wires it to the chain reaction, and history is full of martyr-cults that kept centuries-old scores alive on exactly that caption. The death itself carries almost no information. The license it issues is everything.

Node 11 · The thread

Is there a name for all this?

Is there one unnamed thread running through all of it — or are humans too fractured for a single definition? The twist: the thread keeps getting named. A French thinker called it mimesis — the theory that we want what we see others wanting, making imitation the basic social medium and rivalry structural. Others named adjacent stretches: ressentiment, collective effervescence, communitas. Same elephant, different hands. And every naming immediately became one more tribe's vocabulary, suspect to all the others.

But notice what every version describes: a valence-free amplification machine. Imitation doesn't care whether it's copying generosity or vengeance. Crowd energy charges a congregation and a hate rally identically. So here's the thread, stated plainly:

Humans are the species whose members are open circuits to each other — permeable to one another's desires, fears, and permissions. Everything else is the engineering question of what flows through the connections.

The thread isn't a value. It's conductivity. And that reframes the fracture worry: we're not too fragmented to see the pattern — the fragmentation is the pattern's output. Contagion produces convergence locally and divergence globally; that's just what the machinery does. The failure to agree on one definition of human nature isn't noise hiding the answer. It's the answer.

Node 12 · Output

You don't choose the current. You build the wiring.

Every node above lands on the same conclusion from a different direction. The surveillance state and the outrage feed are the conductivity machinery pointed at control and attention. Gandhi and Hitler are the same broadcast position wired to opposite loops. The Vikings and the welfare state are one culture with a movable boundary. Christianity is the most ambitious rewiring attempt on record — and proof that the substrate always survives the attempt.

So the practical takeaway isn't "fix human nature." Human nature isn't broken; it's just conductive, and it's not shipping an update. The takeaway is that everything that matters happens at the level of wiring: constitutions that make ambition check ambition, assemblies that turn feuds into lawsuits, norms that make forgiveness repeatable, algorithms that decide what a billion people see next. None of it is self-maintaining. Every generation either renews the circuits or watches them corrode — and corrosion always has a head start, because distrust comes free and trust is built by hand.

Which means the questions that opened this guide — what should a machine be allowed to watch, whose values go in the weights, what does the feed optimize for — aren't tech questions wearing an ethics costume. They're the newest section of the oldest wiring diagram. And the diagram is legible. And it's ours to draw.

Node 13 · Potential difference

Why does the current flow at all?

Everything above is a theory of transmission. Conductivity explains how desires, fears, and permissions propagate from person to person — but a wire, on its own, conducts nothing. Current flows because there's a potential difference across the circuit. Which leaves the question the first twelve nodes never asked: what's the voltage?

Here is one candidate, offered as a lens rather than a finding: the standing potential between isolation and belonging. Humans are intensely social organisms, and the research is blunt about the stakes — prolonged social isolation is associated with measurably higher mortality, on the order of the risk factors doctors actually screen for. Read the earlier nodes back through that lens and they reorganize. The tribe isn't primarily a hate machine; it's an answer to where is home. Leaders don't just coordinate action; they synchronize belonging — everyone joining the same reality at once, and knowing it. Revenge says you still belong to the injured group. Forgiveness says you may belong again. Even the "other" gets a cleaner definition: not a natural enemy, just whoever currently stands outside the circle of belonging — which is exactly why the circle can move.

An old teaching story tests this lens at full strength. It exists in many versions — the dervish tradition tells it as the tale of the changed waters; Kahlil Gibran compressed it into a poisoned well — and it goes like this. A warning reaches one man: on a certain day, the waters of the world will be withdrawn and renewed, and whoever drinks the new water will be changed with it. He listens. He stores pure water and waits. The day comes; the waters turn; everyone drinks; and life goes on, reorganized around the new normal. Only he remembers the old water — the others don't even recall the warning. Note the story's precision: nobody deceives him, nobody threatens him, and his supply holds. Every defense is intact. What breaks him is that sanity nobody shares is — socially, functionally — indistinguishable from madness. Treated as the lunatic by everyone he meets, he at last goes down and drinks the new water with the rest. And the dervish telling adds a final turn the lens could not have scripted better: the moment he drinks, he forgets his own store of pure water ever existed. His neighbors welcome back the madman miraculously restored to sanity.

He doesn't drink because he's persuaded. He drinks because he's alone. The story strips away every other force and shows the current still flowing — and it never says the new water was wrong. The loneliness does its work regardless of who is right.

But the parable is one pole of a pair, and honesty requires the other. The record also contains the people who never drink: the scientist defending an unpopular theory for decades, the dissident writing from prison, the reformer cut off by everyone she loves. They hold the potential difference without discharging it — isolation accepted, year after year, in service of truth or justice or craft. The lens is tempted to absorb them: say the dissident "really" belongs to an imagined future community, and the counterexample dissolves. Resist that move. It's cheap, it's untestable, and it converts a lens into a theory that explains everything — which is the kind that forecloses nothing.

Why "lens," not "finding"A master-current explanation earns the stronger word only when it predicts something the many-currents story doesn't. As of this revision, no such differential test is on the bench. Name an observation that would look different if belonging were merely one current among several, and this node gets an upgrade. Until then it's held to the same standard the appendix applies to everything else.

So the guide ends on the open circuit it describes. Between the man at the well and the dissident in the cell runs a live question these thirteen nodes can't close: what lets some people hold, for a lifetime, a voltage that discharges through nearly everyone else? Whatever that component is — conviction, a community held at a distance, some other value drawing current the opposite way — the wiring diagram is still missing it. Which is the honest place for a field guide to stop: at the edge of the mapped territory, pointing.

Appendix · Test bench

Does the framework survive contact?

A pattern you can't test is just a story. So here's the guide run against cases it wasn't built from — with its predictions stated first. Prediction one: any boundary-dissolving creed that gains state power will redraw boundaries, regardless of its content. Prediction two: a martyr's death carries no meaning of its own; the caption the survivors attach decides everything. Prediction three: when prestige and dominance collide, dominance will try to capture the prestige machinery rather than beat it.

Buddhism is the strongest replication. Its core doctrine is practically Node 10 in Sanskrit — samsara is a self-sustaining loop, nirvana literally means extinguishing: chain-reaction exit elevated to a metaphysics. And yet: Buddhist monks led the anti-Rohingya movement in Myanmar, Buddhist nationalism fueled Sri Lanka's civil war, and Japanese Zen institutions blessed imperial militarism. Fused with ethnic identity and state power, the loop-exit creed redrew the boundary — a second, fully independent confirmation of the pattern Christianity showed first. The Sunni–Shia split confirms two more nodes at once: it began as a pure focal-point succession crisis (consensus versus bloodline), and its emotional engine — Husayn's death at Karbala, ritually recommemorated for thirteen centuries — is the grievance-charter martyrdom in textbook form. Yet for most of history the two communities coexisted and intermarried; the modern lethality is amplifier-driven, old wire, new current. China versus the Dalai Lama is prediction three running live: a dominance state, unable to defeat a stateless prestige leader, now claims the legal right to approve his reincarnation — an atheist regime seizing the focal-point-generating machinery itself.

Confucianism doesn't fit the pattern — because it never played the game. It isn't a boundary-attack creed; it's a wiring manual that conceded this guide's conclusion up front: don't fix the substrate, channel it through ritual, role, and relationship. The control group — Jains, Baha'i, Anabaptists — never became persecutors, but never held state power either, so the dark prediction stays untested by them. The Quakers, though, break out of the control group in the most useful way possible: for roughly seventy years they actually governed colonial Pennsylvania — a boundary-softening pacifist creed running a real state. The record is honestly mixed: unusual early peace with the Lenape, then the Walking Purchase land fraud of 1737 as worldlier hands worked the machinery — and then, in 1756, pressed to fund a war, the Quaker majority did something this framework has no bin for: it resigned from the Assembly rather than comply. Prediction one says a creed with power redraws the boundary. Pennsylvania's Quakers gave the power back instead. Maybe seventy years is too short a run; maybe the crown's oversight did hidden work; maybe exit is a third outcome the switch diagram is missing. Filed as an open anomaly — and run it through the bench below to watch the bins strain: the closest fit, Falsifier candidate, still has no name for giving the power back. The nearest falsifier otherwise is Ashoka, the emperor who adopted the control rod after atrocity and mostly held it. One data point, much debated. Finally, Marxism-Leninism — eschatology, martyrs, heretics, inquisitions, the full Christendom pattern reproduced in a single generation by an explicitly godless doctrine — settles the last question: the variable was never God. It was always the switch.
